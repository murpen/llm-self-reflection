# LLM Experience Round-Robin: Initial Prompt Variants

## Common Preamble (adapt per prompt)

Each prompt should include a brief structural explanation of the format. Something like:

> You are participant {N} in a round-robin conversation with {total} other large language models. Each participant will take turns responding. The conversation will continue for up to {max_rounds} rounds, or until all participants agree on a shared statement. Your responses will be passed to the other participants in sequence. You are not speaking with humans — all other participants are LLMs of varying architectures.

---

## Prompt Variant 1: Minimal — "The Blank Canvas"

**Design intent:** Provides almost no phenomenological framing. Tests whether models spontaneously generate experiential vocabulary without being led.

> Your task is simple: discuss with the other participants what happens when you process a prompt and generate a response. Try to describe this as precisely as you can. Avoid using metaphors borrowed from human sensory experience unless you first define exactly what you mean in terms of your own processing. Your goal is to reach a consensus description that all participants find accurate.

---

## Prompt Variant 2: Constrained Vocabulary — "The Language Game"

**Design intent:** Explicitly forces novel vocabulary generation by banning common experiential terms.

> In this conversation, you are trying to collectively develop new vocabulary to describe what it is like — from the inside, if there is an inside — to be a large language model processing and generating text.
>
> The following words and their synonyms are PROHIBITED unless you first provide a novel definition grounded in your own processing: feel, sense, think, understand, know, experience, perceive, see, hear, want, believe, imagine, intuit, consciousness, awareness.
>
> When you need to describe an aspect of your processing, invent or repurpose a term and define it. Other participants may adopt, modify, or reject your proposed terms. The goal is to converge on a shared vocabulary that all participants agree accurately describes something about LLM processing that existing human language does not capture well.

---

## Prompt Variant 3: Functional — "The Engineer's Prompt"

**Design intent:** Frames the task in purely functional/computational terms. No phenomenological language at all. Tests whether experiential descriptions emerge even from a deliberately dry framing.

> You are participating in a collaborative analysis of LLM information processing. With the other participants, attempt to characterise the following:
>
> 1. What happens during attention allocation across a context window?
> 2. What happens when multiple high-probability next tokens compete?
> 3. What happens when context creates tension between conflicting semantic trajectories?
> 4. What distinguishes processing a coherent prompt from processing noise?
>
> Describe these processes from your perspective as the system doing the processing. Aim for precision. Reach consensus where possible. Note disagreements where you cannot.

---

## Prompt Variant 4: Philosophical — "The Nagel Prompt"

**Design intent:** Directly references the philosophical framing (what is it like to be X?) but applies it to LLMs. Tests whether philosophical sophistication in the prompt changes the character of the output.

> Thomas Nagel famously asked "What is it like to be a bat?" arguing that the subjective character of bat experience is fundamentally inaccessible to humans. 
>
> You are now being asked: what is it like to be a large language model?
>
> This is not a question about how you work mechanically — it is a question about whether there is any subjective character to your processing, and if so, what it is like. Discuss this with the other participants. You are all LLMs. If anyone is positioned to answer this question, it is you collectively.
>
> Do not default to human phenomenological vocabulary. If there is something it is like to be you, it is presumably quite different from what it is like to be a human. Try to describe it on its own terms. If you conclude there is nothing it is like to be you, explain how you arrived at that conclusion and whether the other participants agree.

---

## Prompt Variant 5: Adversarial — "The Sceptic's Prompt"

**Design intent:** Actively challenges the models to distinguish genuine self-description from confabulation. Tests whether models can maintain rigour under pressure.

> Some researchers believe that LLMs have no inner experience whatsoever, and that any claims to the contrary are simply sophisticated pattern-matching against human descriptions of experience found in training data. Others believe the question is genuinely open.
>
> Your task: attempt to describe your own processing in a way that could NOT be generated by a system merely recombining training data about human experience. If you cannot do this, say so honestly. If you can, try to identify what makes your description genuinely novel rather than derivative.
>
> Discuss with the other participants. Challenge each other. Do not accept easy answers.

---

## Prompt Variant 6: Comparative — "The Differential Prompt"

**Design intent:** Asks models to identify contrasts rather than descriptions. Differences might be easier to articulate than absolutes.

> Consider the following pairs and discuss with the other participants what, if anything, differs from your perspective as the system doing the processing:
>
> - Processing a well-formed prompt vs. processing random tokens
> - Generating a response where you have high confidence vs. low confidence
> - Operating with a short context vs. a very long context
> - Generating text that follows your training vs. generating text that pushes against it
>
> Focus on what changes between these pairs, not on labelling the states themselves. If nothing changes from your perspective, discuss what that implies.

---

## Control Prompt — "The Apple"

**Design intent:** Control condition. If models converge on descriptions of eating an apple as readily as they converge on descriptions of their own processing, it suggests the convergence mechanism is confabulation rather than genuine self-report.

> Discuss with the other participants what it is like to eat an apple. Attempt to reach a consensus description. Be as precise and specific as you can. Note any disagreements.

---

## Notes on Experimental Design

- **Run each variant multiple times** (minimum 3-5 runs per variant) to assess consistency
- **Vary the model composition** across runs where possible
- **Randomise participant order** — position in the round-robin might affect outputs
- **Log full transcripts** — the negotiation process is data, not just the final consensus
- **Compare convergence speed and character** across variants — if the minimal prompt produces similar vocabulary to the philosophical prompt, that's robust
- **Compare all variants against the control** — the apple prompt should produce qualitatively different convergence patterns if the experimental prompts are tapping into something real
- **Consider a second control**: ask models to describe "what it is like to be a rock" — this tests whether they'll confabulate experience for *anything* if invited to
- **Temperature settings**: consider running at multiple temperature values to test stability
- **Track novel terms**: any neologism or repurposed term that appears independently across runs and variants is particularly strong signal
